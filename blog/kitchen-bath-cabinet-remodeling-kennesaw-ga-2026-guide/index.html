What Is the Difference Between Cabinet Refacing and Full Replacement?

Cabinet refacing is the process of replacing only the visible doors, drawer fronts, and end panels while keeping the existing cabinet boxes; full replacement removes and replaces everything.

Refacing saves 40-60% versus full replacement and finishes in 3-5 days, but requires structurally sound existing boxes.

Refacing vs. full replacement: refacing is the better option because it preserves usable cabinet boxes, generates less landfill waste, and finishes in under a week — provided your existing carcasses are plumb, square, and water-damage-free. Full replacement is the tradeoff option because it costs significantly more and disrupts your kitchen for 2-6 weeks, but it lets you reconfigure layout, add soft-close hardware, and upgrade to deeper drawers.

Experts at Cabinet IQ Of Kennesaw recommend a refacing assessment before any quote — if box interiors show delamination, water staining, or warping greater than 1/8 inch, replacement becomes the more cost-effective long-term choice.

How Long Does Kitchen Cabinet Installation Take?

Kitchen cabinet installation is the sequence of demolition, prep, delivery, mounting, alignment, and finish work needed to install new or replacement cabinetry.

A typical Kennesaw kitchen cabinet installation takes 2 to 6 weeks from demolition to final hardware, depending on scope.

A galley kitchen (a narrow kitchen with cabinets on two parallel walls) installs faster — usually 10-14 days — than an L-shape or U-shape with an island, which can run 4-6 weeks. Custom cabinetry adds 6-10 weeks of lead time before installation even begins because each piece is built to order.

The cabinet remodeling process

  1. Step 1: In-home consultation. A designer measures the space, discusses layout goals, and reviews door styles and finishes. Typically 60-90 minutes.
  2. Step 2: Design and quote. CAD drawings and a written quote follow within 5-10 business days.
  3. Step 3: Material ordering. Stock cabinets ship in 1-2 weeks; semi-custom in 4-6 weeks; custom in 8-12 weeks.
  4. Step 4: Demolition and prep. Old cabinets removed; walls and local services inspected; floors protected. 1-3 days.
  5. Step 5: Installation. Boxes hung, leveled, scribed to walls; doors and drawers fitted. 3-10 days.
  6. Step 6: Finish and punch list. Hardware, toe kicks, crown molding, and final adjustments. 1-2 days.

What Credentials Should a Cabinet Remodeler in Georgia Have?

Credentials are the licenses, insurance policies, and trade certifications that prove a remodeling contractor is legally and professionally qualified to perform work in Georgia.

Legitimate cabinet remodelers in Georgia must hold a state residential contractor license for projects over $2,500, plus general liability insurance of at least $500,000.

Required and recommended credentials

  • Georgia Residential Basic Contractor License — required by O.C.G.A. § 43-41 for residential jobs over $2,500. Verify at the Georgia State Licensing Board for Residential and General Contractors.
  • General liability insurance — minimum $500,000; $1,000,000 preferred.
  • Workers' compensation — required for any contractor with three or more employees under Georgia law.
  • NKBA membership — the National Kitchen and Bath Association (a trade body for kitchen and bath designers) signals continuing education. Verify at nkba.org.
  • Certified Kitchen Designer (CKD) credential — issued by NKBA after seven years of documented experience and an exam.

Why Does Kennesaw's Climate Affect Cabinet Material Choices?

Climate-driven cabinet selection is the practice of choosing materials and finishes that resist humidity, temperature swings, and regional moisture patterns.

Kennesaw's humid subtropical climate makes moisture-resistant plywood boxes and conversion-varnish finishes the smarter choice over particleboard and lacquer.

Kennesaw sits in a humid subtropical zone with annual average relative humidity above 70% and July highs near 89°F, according to NOAA's National Centers for Environmental Information (source: ncei.noaa.gov). Cabinet boxes made of standard particleboard absorb moisture, swell at the joints, and fail within 8-12 years in unconditioned kitchens. Marine-grade plywood and properly sealed MDF doors hold up significantly better.
