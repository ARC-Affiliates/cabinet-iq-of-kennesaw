What Is Cabinet Refinishing and How Does It Differ From Refacing?

Cabinet refinishing is a finish-only restoration that keeps the original boxes, doors, and drawer fronts in place while applying new stain, paint, or clear coat. It differs from refacing, which replaces door and drawer fronts and adds a veneer skin to the box exteriors.

Refinishing re-coats what you already have; refacing replaces visible surfaces while keeping the boxes.

The team at Cabinet IQ Of Kennesaw (a kitchen cabinet design business in Kennesaw, GA) sees three common scenarios in homes around Kennesaw (a Cobb County city of roughly 35,000 residents along I-75, ZIP codes 30144 and 30152): outdated oak that homeowners want painted white, honey maple that needs to read modern, and 1990s cherry that has lost its sheen.

HVLP spray (High Volume Low Pressure — a spray technique that atomizes finish at low air pressure for a smooth, factory-grade coat) is the standard for quality refinishing work. Brush-and-roll application leaves texture and does not match the look of new cabinetry.

Refinishing vs. refacing vs. replacement

Refinishing vs refacing: refinishing wins on price because you keep all existing components, while refacing wins on style flexibility because you get new door profiles. Refacing is the tradeoff choice when door styles look dated even after a fresh coat.

What Are the Steps in the Cabinet Refinishing Process?

The cabinet refinishing process is a sequence of removal, preparation, finishing, and reinstallation steps performed in a specific order to ensure adhesion and durability.

There are six core steps: assess, remove, clean, sand, finish, and reinstall.

  1. Step 1: Assessment — A finisher inspects box material, hinge condition, and door substrate to confirm refinishing is viable.
  2. Step 2: Removal and labeling — Doors, drawer fronts, and hardware come off and get numbered for exact reinstallation.
  3. Step 3: Degrease and clean — Surfaces are cleaned with a TSP-substitute to remove kitchen grease that would block adhesion.
  4. Step 4: Sand and prime — All surfaces are sanded to 180–220 grit and primed with a bonding primer suited to the existing finish.
  5. Step 5: Spray finish — Two to three coats of conversion varnish or pre-catalyzed lacquer are applied with HVLP equipment.
  6. Step 6: Cure and reinstall — Doors cure 24–48 hours, then get rehung with adjusted hinges and new or polished hardware.
"Proper surface preparation accounts for 80 percent of the success of any finishing project. Cleaning, sanding, and priming determine whether the topcoat lasts five years or fifteen."— Kitchen Cabinet Manufacturers Association, kcma.org

Which Cabinets Can Actually Be Refinished?

Refinishable cabinets are those built with materials that accept primer and topcoat adhesion. Solid wood, quality plywood boxes, and properly prepped MDF doors qualify; thermofoil, laminate, and particle-board fronts generally do not.

Solid wood and good plywood refinish well; thermofoil and cheap particle board do not.

  • Excellent candidates: oak, maple, cherry, birch, hickory — solid wood or plywood
  • Good candidates: paint-grade MDF doors with intact edges
  • Poor candidates: thermofoil (vinyl wrap), high-pressure laminate, water-damaged particle board
  • Replace, do not refinish: any cabinet box with delamination, mold, or structural sag

Myths and facts about cabinet refinishing

Myth: Refinished cabinets won't hold up like new ones.

Fact: A catalyzed conversion varnish is harder and more chemical-resistant than most factory finishes on stock cabinets.

Myth: You can paint cabinets yourself for $200 and get the same result.

Fact: DIY brush-and-roll lacks the atomization of HVLP spray; the texture and durability are visibly different within 12 months.

Myth: Oak grain always shows through paint.

Fact: Grain filler plus high-build primer eliminates grain telegraph when applied correctly.

Myth: Refinishing only works for paint, not stain changes.

Fact: Stain-grade refinishing can shift tone within the same wood family — for example, honey oak to a darker walnut tone.
