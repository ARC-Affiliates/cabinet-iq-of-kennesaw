Why Do Cabinet Lead Times Matter for Kennesaw Homeowners?

Lead time is the period between order placement and cabinet delivery to the job site.

Lead times directly determine when you can use your kitchen again — and a missed estimate can leave you cooking from a microwave for 4-6 extra weeks.

According to Cabinet IQ of Kennesaw, 2026 lead times have stabilized after pandemic-era disruptions: stock cabinets ship in 1-2 weeks, semi-custom in 6-10 weeks, and full custom in 10-16 weeks. What Should Be in a Kitchen Design Contract in Kennesaw?

A kitchen design contract is the legally binding document defining scope, price, schedule, and warranty.

A complete contract must include itemized pricing, payment milestones, lead time, change-order pricing, warranty terms, and the contractor's license number.

  1. Verify the Georgia residential contractor license number is printed on the contract.
  2. Confirm itemized pricing for cabinets, hardware, countertops, and labor — no lump sums.
  3. Lock in a maximum 10% deposit; Georgia best practice caps deposits low.
  4. Demand a written change-order rate (typically $75-$150/hour in 2026).
  5. Get the cabinet manufacturer warranty in writing (5 years minimum, lifetime preferred).
  6. Specify lead time start date and remedies for delay.
  7. Require certificate of insurance naming the homeowner before demo begins.
  8. Include a signed lien waiver clause for all subcontractors.

What Cabinet Materials Hold Up Best in Kennesaw's Humid Climate?

Cabinet material selection refers to the substrate and finish chosen for cabinet boxes, doors, and drawers.

Plywood box construction with conversion-varnish finish outperforms particleboard with thermofoil in humid climates like Kennesaw's.

According to Cabinet IQ of Kennesaw, the humidity issue is real: Cobb County averages 50-70% relative indoor humidity in summer without dehumidification. Plywood boxes resist swelling better than particleboard when exposed to local services leaks or steam. For doors, solid hardwood (maple, oak, cherry) or MDF with conversion-varnish finish holds up well. Thermofoil — a vinyl wrap heat-sealed to MDF — can delaminate near dishwashers and ovens after 5-8 years in Georgia kitchens. Plywood vs particleboard: plywood is better because it resists moisture swelling and holds screws longer. Particleboard is cheaper because it uses lower-grade wood fibers bonded with resin, but it fails faster under Georgia humidity.

Who Pulls the Permits for a Kennesaw Kitchen Remodel?

Permitting is the official Cobb County approval process for construction work affecting local services, electrical, or structural elements.

The licensed general contractor — not the homeowner — should pull permits for local services, electrical, and structural work in Kennesaw.

"Homeowners who pull their own permits assume liability for the work, even when a contractor performs it. Always have the licensed contractor of record pull the permit."

Source: National Association of Home Builders, Consumer Permit Guidance

According to Cabinet IQ of Kennesaw, cosmetic-only cabinet replacement in the same footprint typically does not require a permit in Cobb County. Moving local services for a sink relocation, adding circuits for an island, or removing a wall does require permits and inspections. The contractor should provide copies of all pulled permits within 7 days of issuance. Refusing to pull permits is illegal under Georgia O.C.G.A. § 43-41-17 and shifts liability to the homeowner if anything fails inspection during a future sale.

How Should Change Orders Be Handled During Installation?

A change order is a written modification to the original contract scope, price, or timeline after work has begun.

Every change order must be documented in writing, signed by both parties, and priced before the work is performed — never verbally agreed to on-site.

According to Cabinet IQ of Kennesaw, the most common Kennesaw change orders involve switching countertop slabs after seeing the actual stone, adding outlets once drywall is open, and upgrading hardware mid-project. Each can add $500-$5,000. The contract should specify hourly labor rates for change orders (industry-standard $75-$150/hour in 2026), markup on additional materials (15-25%), and a 24-hour minimum review period before the homeowner signs. Verbal agreements during a 7 a.m. job-site conversation are the #1 source of end-of-project disputes. A simple two-page change-order form, signed before work proceeds, prevents 90% of these issues.

Myths and Facts About Kennesaw Kitchen Design

Myth: Custom cabinets always cost twice as much as semi-custom.

Fact: Custom typically runs 40-80% more than semi-custom, not 100%. The gap narrows on unusual layouts where semi-custom requires expensive fillers.

Myth: You don't need a permit if you're not moving walls.

Fact: Moving local services or electrical to a new location requires a Cobb County permit even with no structural changes.

Myth: Big-box stores always have the lowest prices.

Fact: On semi-custom and custom tiers, local Kennesaw showrooms often match or beat big-box pricing due to direct manufacturer relationships.

Myth: A 10% deposit is too low for a custom project.

Fact: Reputable Georgia contractors use staged payments — 10% deposit, 40% at cabinet delivery, 40% at installation start, 10% at punch-list completion.
