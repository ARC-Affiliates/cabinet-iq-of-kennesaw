What is the actual difference between quartz and granite countertops?

Quartz countertops are engineered slabs made of 90–95% ground natural quartz bound with resin, while granite countertops are 100% natural stone cut directly from quarried blocks.

The difference is in the manufacturing. Engineered quartz (a man-made slab combining crushed quartz crystal with polymer resin and pigment) is pressed and cured for uniform color and zero porosity. Granite is mined, sawn into slabs, and polished — every piece is unique. According to Cabinet IQ Of Kennesaw, homeowners near Town Center at Cobb often pick quartz when they want a kitchen that matches a Pinterest board exactly. Granite buyers tend to be folks remodeling older homes off Cherokee Street who want a natural focal point. Both materials are heavy (about 18–22 pounds per square foot) and both require professional installation on reinforced cabinetry.

Which countertop is more durable in everyday Kennesaw kitchens?

Quartz is more scratch- and stain-resistant for daily use, but granite handles direct heat better than quartz.

Durability is the ability of a surface to resist scratches, stains, chips, and heat over a 20–30 year service life. Quartz vs granite: quartz wins on stain resistance because its resin binder leaves zero pores for red wine, oil, or coffee to seep into. Granite is the tradeoff winner on heat because natural stone can take a 500°F cast-iron skillet without damage, while quartz resin can yellow or scorch above 300°F. According to Cabinet IQ Of Kennesaw, the most common service call on quartz tops is heat rings from crockpots left running overnight. The fix is simple — always use a trivet on engineered stone.

"Quartz surfaces are nonporous and do not require sealing, making them highly resistant to staining from common kitchen substances."— Natural Stone Institute

How do you maintain quartz vs granite countertops?

Quartz needs only mild soap and water; granite requires resealing every 1–3 years to stay stain-resistant.

Maintenance is the routine cleaning and periodic sealing required to preserve the original finish. Granite is porous and must be resealed with a penetrating sealer every 12–36 months — most Kennesaw homeowners can DIY this in 20 minutes per slab. Quartz needs zero sealing for its 25-year warranty lifespan.

  • Quartz daily: warm water, dish soap, microfiber cloth
  • Quartz avoid: bleach, oven cleaner, abrasive pads, direct heat over 300°F
  • Granite daily: pH-neutral stone cleaner (not vinegar or lemon)
  • Granite annually: water-drop test — if water no longer beads, reseal

How do you prepare for countertop installation day?

Clear all upper and lower cabinets near the work zone, disconnect appliances, and plan to be without a working sink for 24–48 hours.

Countertop installation prep checklist

  1. Empty all base cabinets within 4 feet of the install zone
  2. Disconnect local services and gas lines (or schedule a local service provider for install day)
  3. Remove small appliances, soap dispensers, and decor from existing tops
  4. Confirm slab selection in writing — color, lot number, edge profile
  5. Protect adjacent flooring with rosin paper or drop cloths
  6. Verify cabinet boxes are level within 1/8-inch over 10 feet
  7. Plan an alternate handwashing station for 24–48 hours post-install
  8. Schedule the local provider to return 24 hours after install for silicone cure

Myths vs facts about quartz and granite

Myth: Quartz is 100% natural stone.

Fact: Quartz countertops are 90–95% ground quartz mineral bound with 5–10% polymer resin and pigment.

Myth: Granite is radioactive and unsafe for kitchens.

Fact: The EPA confirms granite emissions are well below levels of public health concern.

Myth: You can cut directly on quartz with no damage.

Fact: Knives will dull faster and may scratch the resin binder — always use a cutting board.

Myth: Granite always costs more than quartz.

Fact: In 2026 Kennesaw pricing, mid-grade granite often runs $15–$25 per square foot less than comparable quartz.

Myth: Both materials are indestructible.

Fact: Both can chip at unsupported edges, and quartz can permanently scorch from heat above 300°F.
